# Divinr Personas

**Last updated**: 2026-04-19

The three personas Divinr is built for, in plain prose. Used for sanity-checking feature decisions and marketing copy — if a proposed change doesn't improve the experience for at least one of these people, it probably doesn't belong.

---

## The St. Thomas student

A business or finance undergraduate encountering Divinr through a classroom or club partnership at St. Thomas. They're learning how to read markets and want structured practice without real-money risk. They enter through a club invite, join the associated curriculum, and spend most of their time in the club activity feed, the curriculum walkthroughs, and paper-trading tournaments. They care about seeing how the AI analysts reason, comparing their own conviction to an analyst's, and earning visible progress (tour completion, leaderboard rank, mentor feedback). They are not power users — they should never have to open the contract editor or touch a triple slot.

## The builder-type power user

A technical user (the golfergeek archetype — software engineer or quant-adjacent) who wants to author their own analysts, plug in their own LLM credentials, and see cost-modeling dashboards before deciding what to run. They enter through the landing page or a direct invite, skip most of the onboarding tour, and go straight to the analyst contract editor, triple-slot management, and cost breakdown. They care about transparent reasoning, BYO-model flexibility, per-analyst margin analysis, and a clear path from "my custom analyst is converging" to "my custom analyst is promoted to base." They'll read `docs/features.md` and expect it to be current.

## The casual-curious beta tester

A finance-curious professional who signed up during beta to see what "AI market analysis you can actually understand" looks like. They skim the landing page, join through an invite link, skip most configuration, and spend their first session poking at the dashboard, the analyst modal, and a tournament leaderboard. They don't want to become an analyst author; they want to feel like the product is credible, transparent, and fun. They care about reading a clean analyst rationale, watching paper P&L move, and maybe joining a club. If the disclaimers, copy, or UI feel sloppy on first look, they churn before session two.
